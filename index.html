Understanding the Basics

What is IDD?

Intellectual and Developmental Disability (IDD) refers to differences that affect a person’s learning, reasoning, and everyday life skills from an early age. People with IDD are lifelong learners who benefit from supportive environments that build on their strengths.

Strengths and Supports

Common Characteristics and Needs

Many learners with IDD thrive with predictable routines, clear instructions, and patient guidance. They may experience challenges in communication or problem-solving, but they also bring creativity, loyalty, and unique perspectives to the classroom.

  • Respond well to visual cues and step-by-step directions.
  • Benefit from hands-on activities and real-life examples.
  • Need time to process information and practice skills.
  • Flourish when classmates show encouragement and respect.

Teaching for Everyone

Inclusive Education and Classroom Strategies

Teachers can create welcoming spaces where every learner belongs. These strategies support students with IDD while benefiting the whole class.

Differentiated Instruction

Offer multiple ways to learn—videos, visuals, demonstrations, and practice.

Visual Supports

Use schedules, icons, and color-coding to make steps clear.

Peer Buddies

Pair students for activities that encourage friendship and collaboration.

Predictable Routines

Start and end class with consistent rituals to build confidence.

Call to Action

Our Advocacy Goals

We aim to celebrate the talents of people with IDD, encourage inclusive classrooms, and inspire our school community to speak up for accessibility and respect. Everyone can make a difference—by learning more, practicing kindness, and designing lessons that welcome all learners.

Take Action

  • Learn about IDD and share inclusive language with friends.
  • Invite classmates with diverse needs to collaborate on projects.
  • Speak up for accessibility, clear instructions, and flexible classrooms.
